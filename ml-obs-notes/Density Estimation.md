Simple distributions have limited capabilities to model the data. **Mixture models** could be more useful for these goals. They can also describe multimodal data (in clusters).

Mixture model is a convex combination of $K$ base distributions
	$p(x)\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^K~\pi_kp_k(x)$ 
	for mixture weights $0 \leqslant \pi_k \leqslant 1, ~~~\displaystyle \sum_{k=1}^K \pi_k =1$ 
	$p_k$ is a basic distribution (e.g. Gaussian, Beta, etc.) 


## Gaussian Mixture Models

GMM is a combination of finite number of Gaussian distributions with
	$p(x|\theta) = \displaystyle \sum_{k=1}^K~\pi_k~\mathcal N (x|\mu_k,\Sigma_k)$ 
	$\theta:= \lbrace \mu_k,\Sigma_k,\pi_k: k=1,...,K \rbrace$ is the collection of the parameters of the model. 

To obtain the maximum likelihood estimate $\theta_{ML}$ for the model, the likelihood is turned into a log
	$p(\mathcal X | Î¸) = \displaystyle\prod_{n=1}^N~p(x_n | Î¸)$ with $p(x_n|\theta)=\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^K~\pi_k ~\mathcal N(x_n|\mu_k,\Sigma_k)$  
	**into log->** $log~p(\mathcal X|\theta)$ $=$$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^N~log~p(x_n|\theta)$ $=$ $\underbrace{\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^N~log\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^K~\pi_k ~\mathcal N(x_n|\mu_k,\Sigma_k)}_{:=\mathcal L}$ 
	$\mathcal L$ - log-likelihood

The goal is to find params $\theta_{ML}^*$ that maximise $\mathcal L$. Here the closed-form solution can not be obtained, so the approach to find good $\theta_{ML}$ is to update one parameter at a time while keeping other fixed.

For any local optimum, its gradient with respect to params is 0. For our case, the necessary conditions to optimise $\mathcal L$ with respect to GMM params $\mu_k, \Sigma_k, \pi_k$ 
	$\cfrac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \mu_k}$ $= 0^âŠ¤ \Leftrightarrow$ $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^N~\cfrac{\partial~log~p(x_n|\theta)}{\partial \mu_k}$ $=$ $0^âŠ¤$
	$\cfrac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \Sigma_k}$ $= 0 \Leftrightarrow$ $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^N~\cfrac{\partial~log~p(x_n|\theta)}{\partial \Sigma_k}$ $=$ $0$ 
	$\cfrac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \pi_k}$ $= 0 \Leftrightarrow$ $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^N~\cfrac{\partial~log~p(x_n|\theta)}{\partial \pi_k}$ $=$ $0$

By applying the chain rule, partial derivative with respect to $\theta$ takes form
	$\cfrac{\partial~log~p(x_n|\theta)}{\partial \theta}$ $=$$\cfrac{1}{p(x_n|\theta)}~\cfrac{\partial~p(x_n|\theta)}{\partial \theta}$ 
	with model params $\theta:= \lbrace \mu_k,\Sigma_k,\pi_k: k=1,...,K \rbrace$
	and
	$\cfrac{1}{p(x_n|\theta)}$$=$$\cfrac{1}{\sum_{j=1}^K~\pi_j~\mathcal N(x_n|\mu_j,\Sigma_j)}$   

### Responsibilities

Responsibility of the $k$th mixture on the $n$th data point is defined as
	$r_{nk}:=\cfrac{\pi_k~\mathcal N(x_n|\mu_k,\Sigma_k)}{\sum_{j=1}^K~\pi_j~\mathcal N(x_n|\mu_j,\Sigma_j)}$ 
It is proportional to the likelihood
	$p(x_n|\pi_k,\mu_k,\Sigma_k)=\pi_k \mathcal N (x_n|\mu_k,\Sigma_k)$ 

Mixture components have high responsibility for a data point when the data point could be a plausible sample from that mixture component. 

$r_n:=[r_{n1},...,r_{nK}]^âŠ¤ \in \mathbb R^K$ is a (normalised) probability vector, i.e. $\sum_k r_{nk}=1$ for $r_{nk} \geqslant 0$. $r_n$ is sort of an assignment of $x_n$ to the $K$ mixture components. 

> So, $r_{nk}$ represents the probability that $x_n$ has been generated by the $k$th mixture component.

As said before, the optimal parameters $\mu_k, \Sigma_k, \pi_k$ are found by optimising one component while keeping others fixed.

### Mean update
For the mean $\mu_k, k=1,...,K$, the update of the GMM is given by
	$\mu_k^{new}=\cfrac{\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^N~r_{nk}x_n}{\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^N~r_{nk}}$ 
	The closed form solution for all $\mu_k$ can not be found.

This can be interpreted as an importance-weighed estimate. Importance of data point $x_n$ is the responsibility $r_{nk}$ of the $k$th cluster for $x_n,~k=1,...,K$. 
Mean $\mu_k$ is pulled toward a data point $x_n$ with strength $r_{nk}$ . The update can be also interpreted as expected value of all data points under the distribution given by
	$r_k:=\cfrac{[r_{1k},...,r_{Nk}]^âŠ¤}{N_k}$ 

### Covariance update
For the covariance parameters $\Sigma_k, k=1,...,K$, the update of the GMM is given by
	$\Sigma_k^{new}=\cfrac{1}{N_k}\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^N~r_{nk}(x_n-\mu_k)(x_n-\mu_k)^âŠ¤$ 
	with $r_{nk}$ as before
	and
	$N_k:=\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^N~r_{nk}$ 

This also can be seen as a Monte Carlo estimate of $x_n$ associated with $k$th component, weighted by $r_{nk}$.

### Weights update
The mixture weights of the GMM are updated as
	$\pi_k^{new}=\cfrac{N_k}{N},~~~k=1,...,K$ 
	$N$ - number of data points
	$N_k$ as before

The mixture's weight is the ratio of the total responsibility of the $k$th cluster and the number of data points.

## Expectation Maximisation (EM)

The EM algorithm is a general iterative scheme for learning parameters (MLE/MAP) in mixture models and latent-variable models.

In the example of a Gaussian mixture model, the initial $\mu_k, \Sigma_k, \pi_k$ are chosen, and alternated until convergence between the E-step and M-step
	E-step: Evaluate the responsibilities $r_{nk}$ (posterior probability of data point $n$ belonging to the mixture component $k$)
	M-step: Use the updated responsibilities to reestimate the $\mu_k, \Sigma_k, \pi_k$

Every step in the algorithm increases the log-likelihood. For convergence, either the log-likelihood or the params directly can be checked.

In GMM updates, the updated means $\mu_k$ are used in the following updates of $\Sigma_k$ and $\pi_k$. 

## Latent-Variable Perspective

GMM can be seen from the perspective of a discrete latent-var $z$. This is contrasting to PCA, where latent-vars were continuous in $\mathbb R^M$. 

Assuming a mixture model with $K$ components and that a data point $x$ can be generated only by a one component. Then, a binary var $z_k \in \lbrace 0,1 \rbrace$ indicates whether the $k$th mixture component generated that data point.
	$p(x|z_k=1)=\mathcal N(x|\mu_k,\Sigma_k)$ 

$z:=[z_1,...,z_K]^âŠ¤ \in \mathbb R^K$ is a probability vector consisting of $K-1$ 0s and exactly one 1. (one-hot encoding / 1-of-K representation)

The $z_k$ are not known, therefore a prior is placed on the latent-var $z$ 
	$p(z)=\pi=[\pi_1,...,\pi_K]^âŠ¤,~~~\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^K~\pi_k=1$ 
	$\pi_k=p(z_k=1)$ 

The sampling procedure for $z$ is quite simple
1. Sample $z^{(i)} \sim p(z)$ 
2. Sample $x^{(i)} \sim p(x|z^{(i)}=1)$ 

The joint distribution of the model is
	$p(x,z_k=1) = p(x|z_k=1)p(z_k=1)=\pi_k ~ \mathcal N(x|\mu_k, \Sigma_k)$ 
	and
	$p(x,z) =$ $\begin{bmatrix} p(x,z_1=1) \\ \vdots \\ p(x,z_K=1) \end{bmatrix}$ $=$ $\begin{bmatrix} \pi_1~\mathcal N (x|\mu_1,\Sigma_1) \\ \vdots \\ \pi_K~\mathcal N (x|\mu_K,\Sigma_K) \end{bmatrix}$ 

To obtain the likelihood $p(x|\theta)$, the latent-vars need to be marginalised. This can be done by summing out all latent vars from the joint $p(x,z)$ ($\sum$, since discrete)
	$p(x|\theta)=\displaystyle\sum_z~p(x|\theta,z)p(z|\theta)$
	$\theta:=\lbrace \mu_k,\Sigma_k,\pi_k:~~k=1,...,K \rbrace$ 

There are only $K$ possible configuration settings of $z$, since there is only a single nonzero entry in each $z$

Summing over all $z$ is equal to looking at the nonzero entry of the $z$-vector
	**$p(x|\theta)=\displaystyle\sum_z~p(x|\theta,z)p(z|\theta)$ 
	$=\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^K~p(x|\theta,z_k=1)p(z_k=1|\theta)$ 
	$=\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^K~\pi_k~\mathcal N(x|\mu_k,\Sigma_k)$ 
	 
Given the dataset, the likelihood is
	$p(\mathcal X|\theta)=\displaystyle\prod_{n=1}^N~p(x_n|\theta)$ 
	$=$$\displaystyle\prod_{n=1}^N~\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^K~\pi_k~\mathcal N(x_n|\mu_k,\Sigma_k)$ 

Posterior distribution on the latent-var $z$ of the $k$th component having generated data point $x$
	$p(z_k=1|x)=\cfrac{p(z_k=1)p(x|z+k=1)}{p(x)}$ 

The distribution is then
	$p(z_k=1|x)=\cfrac{p(z_k = 1)p(x | z_k = 1)}{\sum_{j=1}^K~p(z_j=1)p(x|z_j=1)}$ $=$$\cfrac{\pi_k~\mathcal N(x|\mu_k,\Sigma_k)}{\sum_{j=1}^K~\pi_j~\mathcal N(x|\mu_j,\Sigma_j)}$ 

To extend the modelling to the whole dataset, conditional distribution can be factorised
	$p(x_1,...,x_N|z_1,...,z_N)$ $=$$\displaystyle\prod_{n=1}^N~p(x_n|z_n)$ 

And posterior probability that the $k$th component generated $x$ corresponds to the responsibility $r_{nk}$
	$p(z_{nk}=1|x_n)=r_{nk}$ 


EM algorithm can be principally derived from latent-var perspective. Given current setting of params $\theta^{(t)}$, the E-Step calculates the expected log-likelihood
	$\mathcal Q(\theta |\theta^{(t)}) = \mathbb E_{z|x,\theta^{(t)}}[log~p(x,z|\theta)]$ $=\int~log~p(x,z|\theta)p(z|x,\theta^{(t)})dz$  
		expectation of $log~p(x,z|\theta)$ is taken with respect to the posterior $p(z|x,\theta^{(t)})$ of the latent-vars

The M-Step selects an updated set of params $\theta^{(t+1)}$ by maximising (via integrating)

With unknown number of components $K$, nested cross-validation

## Non-parametric density estimation

There are also non-parametric ways of density estimation

**Histograms** are constructed by binning the data space and count, how many data points fall into each bin. Then a bar is drawn at the center of each bin, representing the count (produces unsmoothed count)

Kernel density estimation is another nonparametric way of density estimation. 

Given $N$ i.i.d. samples, KDE represents the underlying distribution as
	$p(x)=\cfrac{1}{Nh}\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^Nk~(\cfrac{x-x_n}{h})$ 
	$k$ is a kernel function (non-neg) that integrates to 1 
	$h>0$ is a smoothing/bandwidth parameter (similar role as size in histograms)
Kernel is placed on every single data point $x_n$ in the data set.
